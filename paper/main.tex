\begin{abstract}
\textit{Background and gap.} Regime models underpin capital, risk-limit, and hedging decisions, yet existing literature evaluates one fitted specification at a time. Basel~IV and SR~11-7 require structural stability across admissible specifications.
\textit{Contribution.} We provide the first systematic audit of representation invariance for discrete-state regime models, measuring partition agreement by ARI and coarse risk-ordering agreement by a matching-free rank-aligned metric calibrated against a $1/K$ null.
\textit{Method.} We test four single-asset case studies under four model classes (HMM, GMM, k-means, spectral) at $K\in\{2,3,4,5\}$. Eight representations (seven for all assets, one VIX-based for S\&P~500 only) vary in standardisation, feature subset, rolling horizon, volatility estimator, and information source. A step-size sweep separates regime persistence from window overlap.
\textit{Findings.} Cross-representation ARI is $0.34$--$0.43$ across all assets under HMM and GMM (with non-parametric robustness benchmarks confirming the same pattern at $0.29$--$0.47$), well below the $0.65$ poor-recovery threshold; disagreement persists under k-means and at non-overlapping windows. Top-1 ordering Jaccard at $K=3$ is $0.47$ against a $1/K=0.20$ null, a $2.4\times$ excess comparable to the partition signal.
\textit{Significance.} Representation-conditional CVaR diverges by $39$--$73\%$ of the unconditional CVaR on average and approaches $300\%$ in COVID-19 stress (BTC-USD HMM peak $299\%$). We recommend a cross-representation envelope as a diagnostic complement to temporal backtesting; the registered admissible class (Supplementary Section~\ref{app:scope}) is narrow, so the reported ARI is a conservative upper bound.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Market regimes are widely used in risk assessment and portfolio construction \citep{Hamilton1989}. Under Basel IV and SR 11-7 \citep{SR117}, models must demonstrate structural stability, not merely acceptable backtests. Yet one layer remains largely unaudited: the representation layer, namely the choice of features, rolling windows, and preprocessing rules. Regime assignments depend on how the series is represented \citep{BengioCourvilleVincent2013}, much as factor model inference depends on the choice of characteristics \citep{GuKellyXiu2020,KellyPruittSu2019}. If the same market segment can be labelled differently under equally defensible representations, regime-based capital, hedging, and limit frameworks inherit a source of model risk that standard validation rarely quantifies \citep{DanielssonJamesValenzuelaZer2016}.

The clustering stability literature \citep{BenDavidLuxburgPal06,vonLuxburg2010} formalises stability under a \emph{fixed} representation and algorithm; it does not address stability when the \emph{representation} itself varies. The supervised-learning analog is the underspecification framework of \citet{DAmouretal2022}, but regime inference has no held-out anchor with which to adjudicate between $\Phi_1$ and $\Phi_2$. Recent FRL work treats representation as a fixed input and evaluates performance after the fact \citep{Kirby2023FRL,BlazsekKongShadoff2025,Ampountolas2025FRL,WangYuanQu2026FRL}; \citet{Ampountolas2025FRL} note that feature choice shapes regime assignments, but evaluation uses forecasting performance rather than partition agreement.

This paper instead studies \emph{practical representation-invariant stability}: whether inferred regimes remain stable when feature construction, preprocessing, and windowing vary within an admissible class. We test on four single-asset daily series chosen for governance relevance (\SPX{}, \IEF{}, \GLD{}, \BTC{}) under HMM, GMM, and non-parametric (k-means and spectral-clustering) benchmarks. The contribution is twofold: a partition-level result (cross-representation ARI 0.34--0.43 across all assets under HMM and GMM (with non-parametric benchmarks at 0.29--0.47), well below \citet{Steinley2004}'s 0.65 poor-recovery threshold), and a corrected ordering-level result (an earlier draft claimed near-unity ordering agreement under a Hungarian + CVaR matching rule; we retract that claim, since the matching rule's null is itself $0.41$--$0.99$ across (asset, $K$), and under a matching-free metric with a rigorous $1/K$ null the honest excess is $1.8\times$--$3.4\times$, comparable in magnitude to the partition-level signal, not categorically higher). The admissible class we register (Supplementary Section~\ref{app:scope}) is deliberately near-redundant: five of seven representations share VaR/CVaR tails, four share the same rolling windows. We chose the class most likely to produce high cross-representation agreement, and the partition-instability finding holds even there. Broadening the class can only weaken agreement, so the reported numbers are a conservative upper bound on production-scale agreement (equivalently, a lower bound on disagreement).

Standard asymptotic theory guarantees that two analysts sharing a feature mapping, parametric family, and infinite data recover the same partition \citep{Teicher1963,Hamilton1989,Guidolin2011}, but is silent on analysts who pick \emph{different} admissible mappings on the same finite, non-stationary sample and presumes a correct emission family. The full theoretical development and the empirical-fragility evidence \citep{DanielssonJamesValenzuelaZer2016} are in Supplementary Section~\ref{app:theory}.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Minimal identification environment}

We adopt a minimal single-asset design, applied independently to \BTC{}, \GLD{}, \SPX{}, and \IEF{} so that asset-specific noise can be distinguished from structural instability. If representation-invariant stability fails in this minimal setting under standard risk features and simple models, the governance problem is structural rather than model-specific.

\subsection{Risk-based feature representations}

Latent regimes are inferred from rolling risk features common in downside risk management (realised volatility, drawdown, VaR, CVaR, realised skewness, vol-of-vol). The admissible class is eight mappings (seven applied universally, one \SPX{}-only) varying along five documentation-relevant dimensions: standardisation, feature subset, rolling horizon, volatility estimator (rolling realised vs GARCH(1,1)), and information source (backward-looking realised risk vs forward-looking VIX). Six representations share a rolling-window architecture; rep\_d substitutes GARCH conditional volatility, and rep\_e (\SPX{} only) uses VIX. The class is near-redundant by design: it bundles the specifications most likely to agree, so the reported ARI is the configuration-most-favourable-to-stability case, not a worst case. Any broader admissible class can only add disagreement, so the reported ARI is a conservative \emph{upper bound} on agreement realised in production specification spaces. Conclusions are statements within this registered scope rather than universal claims; per-dimension justification and exact definitions are in Supplementary Sections~\ref{app:scope} and~\ref{app:representations}.

\subsection{State inference models}

We estimate three discrete-state models with $K=3$ in the baseline: a Gaussian HMM \citep{ElliottAggounMoore1995} via \texttt{hmmlearn}, a Gaussian Mixture Model via \texttt{scikit-learn}, and a non-parametric robustness benchmark covering k-means and spectral clustering (Section~\ref{sec:nonparam}) which dispense with Gaussian-emission and centroid-based assumptions respectively. HMM and GMM are the canonical operational regime classifiers \citep{Hamilton1989,AngTimmermann2012,Guidolin2011}. The $K=3$ baseline reflects the practitioner low/medium/high-risk convention; the $K$-sweep in Section~\ref{sec:ksens} confirms the finding holds for $K\in\{2,3,4,5\}$. Continuous-state models (score-driven, stochastic-volatility, copula-based regimes) and deep classifier families lie outside this scope.

\subsection{Practical representation-invariant stability (empirical diagnostic)}
\label{sec:diagnostic}

Let $X$ be an observed series, $\Phi \in \mathcal{F}$ a rolling feature mapping, $\mathcal{M}$ a class of state inference models with $K$ latent states, and $S(\Phi, M) = \mathrm{Infer}(\Phi(X), M)$ the inferred state sequence. Regime labels exhibit \emph{practical representation-invariant stability} over $(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{M})$ if $\inf_{\Phi_1,\Phi_2,M_1,M_2} \mathrm{ARI}\big(S(\Phi_1,M_1), S(\Phi_2,M_2)\big) \ge \delta$ for some $\delta > 0$. We summarise this by the mean cross-representation ARI; the mean understates the infimum, so a mean below $\delta=0.65$ implies the infimum is below as well. The $0.65$ threshold is borrowed from \citet{Steinley2004}'s simulation calibration, which is psychometric rather than financial; Supplementary Section~\ref{app:dialectical}(i) verifies that the partition finding is also below a substantially looser $0.50$ threshold, so the headline conclusion does not turn on the specific cut-off. The diagnostic is descriptive of the registered class: it does not test theoretical identifiability and does not adjudicate between \emph{instability} and \emph{irreducible plurality}; Section~\ref{sec:discussion} returns to this. A closed-form bound from feature-distribution divergence to label ARI is left for future work.

\subsection{Stability diagnostics}

We measure \emph{cross-representation agreement} (pairwise similarity between state sequences from different $\Phi$ within a fixed model class $M$) and \emph{temporal agreement} (between consecutive rolling windows under identical $\Phi$ and $M$). Temporal agreement uses two metrics: an \emph{overlap metric} (labels on the $W-s$ dates shared by consecutive windows; mechanically inflated \citep{vonLuxburg2010}) and a \emph{disjoint metric} (labels on the $s$ dates unique to each window; a lag-$W$ proxy for persistence). The gap quantifies overlap inflation. The primary metric is the Adjusted Rand Index \citep{HubertArabie1985}; AMI \citep{VinhEppsBailey2010} and per-pair tests ($B=99$, iid permutation and a \citet{PolitisRomano1992CBB} circular block bootstrap with Patton-Politis-White block length, evaluated against Benjamini-Hochberg FDR control at $q=0.05$) confirm the same rankings (Supplementary Section~\ref{app:metrics}).

\section{Empirical results}

\subsection{Baseline comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:baseline} reports cross-representation and temporal agreement under the baseline specification (window $=252$, step $=21$, $K=3$, 20 seeds). All cross-rep ARI values fall well below \citet{Steinley2004}'s 0.65 threshold (GMM $0.409$, HMM $0.351$, asset means $0.34$--$0.43$). Per-pair tests reject random labelling for the majority of pairs after Benjamini-Hochberg correction at $q=0.05$ under both iid-permutation and \citet{PolitisRomano1992CBB} circular-block-bootstrap nulls; under the autocorrelation-aware CBB null, $67.7\%$--$71.2\%$ of pairs survive across the four assets (per-asset survival fractions in Supplementary Section~\ref{app:perm_details}). A dimension decomposition (Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:decomp}) shows agreement is highest within feature-subset variation (mean ARI $0.55$) and falls to $0.31$--$0.33$ for information-source, feature-philosophy, or scale variation, so the disagreement tracks the economic content of the specification rather than rolling-window noise; an $\eta^2$ decomposition (Supplementary Section~\ref{app:variance_decomp}) attributes a third of GMM and a fifth of HMM ARI variance to between-pair effects rather than seed noise. The asset-mean spread ($0.37$--$0.40$ across models) is narrow relative to the cross-asset risk-return heterogeneity of \BTC{}/\GLD{}/\SPX{}/\IEF{}, and we do not attempt to attribute the small residual variation to volatility, liquidity, or market-structure differences; the four assets are reported as independent replications rather than a cross-section. Headline numbers are stable on the fully-converged subset: dropping the $5\%$ of HMM fits with $|\Delta\ell|>10^{-3}$ leaves cross-representation and temporal ARI within $\pm 0.005$ of the values in Table~\ref{tab:baseline} (Supplementary Section~\ref{app:convergence}).

\paragraph{Coarse ordering: a corrected reading.}
Earlier drafts claimed that coarse risk ordering was substantially more stable than the partition (Hungarian + CVaR top-1 ${\approx}0.93$ at $K=3$). We retract that claim: the Hungarian rule mechanically rank-aligns CVaR extremes regardless of whether the partitions agree, and the independent-partition null under the same rule is itself $0.41$--$0.99$ across (asset, $K$) (Supplementary Section~\ref{app:ordering}). Under a matching-free rank-aligned metric with a rigorous $1/K$ null, cross-rep top-1 Jaccard at $K=3$ is $0.47$ (null $0.20$, a $2.4\times$ excess), with $1.8\times$--$3.4\times$ excess across $K\in\{2,3,4,5\}$. Coarse ordering is genuinely above chance but at magnitudes comparable to the partition-level ARI, not categorically higher; neither stability dimension is a safe harbour for the other.

\paragraph{Downstream economic impact.}
Label disagreement translates directly into risk-estimate divergence. For each trading day $t$ and representation $\Phi$ we compute regime-conditional 5\% CVaR from in-state log returns within the rolling window; the daily \emph{representation spread} $S_t = \max_\Phi \widehat{\mathrm{CVaR}}_t^\Phi - \min_\Phi \widehat{\mathrm{CVaR}}_t^\Phi$ is expressed as a fraction of unconditional CVaR (Table~\ref{tab:cvar_spread}).

\subsection{State-number sensitivity}
\label{sec:ksens}

We sweep $K \in \{2,3,4,5\}$ (Table~\ref{tab:robustness}). Cross-rep ARI remains below $0.65$ at every $K$ (GMM $0.41$--$0.42$, HMM $0.30$--$0.42$), so the partition finding is not specific to $K=3$. The temporal/cross-rep ranking is $K$-dependent. At $K=2$ both stability dimensions are high but temporal dominates. At $K=4$, GMM cross-rep ARI ($0.415$) exceeds GMM temporal ARI ($0.302$), reversing the $K=3$ ordering. The mechanism is GMM-specific: GMM lacks transition dynamics and assigns observations to mixture components purely on the basis of the input-feature distribution at estimation time, so as $K$ grows the partition becomes finer and a step-size shift of $s=21$ days reassigns a non-trivial fraction of boundary observations, degrading temporal consistency. HMM's transition kernel regularises the partition across consecutive windows and avoids the flip, although both HMM cross-rep ARI ($0.351 \to 0.314$) and HMM temporal ARI ($0.421 \to 0.308$) still drop in tandem from $K=3$ to $K=4$, so the underlying finer-partitions-are-less-stable mechanism is shared across model classes; the apparent ranking flip is a sign-level distinction, not a magnitude one. Full discussion in Supplementary Section~\ref{app:k_sens}. Preprocessing alone shifts partitions: rep\_a vs rep\_a\_unscaled gives ARI ${\approx}0.31$ (Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:decomp}).

\label{sec:nonparam}

Both use \texttt{sklearn} defaults at $K=3$, 5 seeds.

The asset-average ranking is spectral $0.329 <$ HMM $0.351 <$ GMM $0.409 <$ k-means $0.452$: the model class with the fewest parametric constraints (spectral) yields the \emph{lowest} cross-rep agreement, while the most constrained partition geometry (k-means convex centroids) yields the highest. The cross-rep finding is therefore not an artefact of Gaussian emissions; if anything, relaxing the parametric structure strengthens it.

\subsection{Instability or plurality?}
\label{sec:discussion}

Unlike supervised underspecification \citep{DAmouretal2022}, regime inference has no held-out performance anchor, so a low cross-representation ARI admits two readings: \emph{instability} (a defect that a ``better'' pipeline would resolve) or \emph{irreducible plurality} (multiple equally valid partitions of a non-stationary process). To discriminate, we run a synthetic experiment with a known Markov DGP ($K=3$, $p=0.97$, $T=2{,}200$): if plurality were the cause, each representation should still recover the true labels. The synthetic admissible class is restricted to four of the eight representations (rep\_a, rep\_a\_unscaled, rep\_b, rep\_c1) because GARCH (rep\_d) and VIX-based (rep\_e) features have no synthetic analogue under the volatility-only DGP. Instead, recovery ARI averages $0.26$ (HMM) and $0.28$ (GMM), comparable to or below the synthetic cross-rep ARI itself ($0.31$--$0.36$); the pipeline cannot recover the true partition even when one exists, consistent with \emph{instability}. The result is insensitive to drift ($\alpha=0$ ablation gives essentially the same numbers; details in Supplementary Section~\ref{app:synth_setup}). The synthetic and real-data cross-rep ARI being of the same order of magnitude further rebuts the counter-argument that cross-representation disagreement is a property of financial-data noise rather than of the pipeline itself. This does not settle the question for real markets, where the DGP is unknown, but it shifts the prior: low ARI in practice is at least partly attributable to genuine inference failure. Whichever interpretation is adopted, the current practice of reporting a single regime classification without cross-representation sensitivity analysis is inadequate. Five further dialectical robustness points (threshold choice, large-step ARI interpretation, ordering nulls, $\eta^2$ contrast, and synthetic-recovery limits) are addressed in Supplementary Section~\ref{app:dialectical}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Across four single-asset case studies, two parametric models (HMM, GMM), non-parametric benchmarks (k-means, spectral), and five sources of representation variation, regime classifications are materially sensitive to admissible specification choices: cross-rep ARI is $0.34$--$0.43$ under HMM and GMM (non-parametric benchmarks $0.29$--$0.47$), well below \citet{Steinley2004}'s $0.65$ threshold, and persists at non-overlapping windows. Coarse risk ordering is genuinely above chance but at comparable magnitude to the partition (top-1 Jaccard $0.47$ vs $1/K=0.20$), not categorically higher. The economic stakes are direct: regime-conditional CVaR diverges across representations by $39$--$73\%$ of unconditional CVaR on average and approaches $300\%$ during COVID-19 (Table~\ref{tab:cvar_spread}, BTC-USD HMM peak $299\%$), an unquantified model limitation that single-specification backtesting cannot detect under SR 11-7 \citep{SR117}.

\paragraph{Recommendation and workflow.}
We recommend a \emph{representation envelope} as a diagnostic complement to temporal backtesting: under the registered admissible class, compute the regime-conditional risk measure for each $\Phi$ and report the point estimate alongside $[\min_\Phi, \max_\Phi]$. When envelope width exceeds 10\% of the unconditional measure or cross-representation ARI falls below 0.65, escalate to MRM committee and either adopt the conservative representation, form an ensemble, or document the limitation under SR 11-7's known-limitation provisions. Operationally this is a quarterly compute step yielding three numbers (cross-rep ARI, envelope width, worst-asset percentile) against documented triggers. As a worked \SPX{} 2020-Q1 example: on 23 March 2020 four representations assign \SPX{} to the high-volatility state (conditional CVaR ${\sim}{-}700$~bp/day) while two assign the medium state (${\sim}{-}200$~bp/day); the envelope width is ${\approx}500$~bp ($200\%$ of unconditional CVaR) and trailing cross-rep ARI is well below $0.65$, so both triggers fire and the day's classification escalates with two orders of magnitude of headroom above the materiality threshold (full walkthrough in Supplementary Section~\ref{app:worked_example}). Implementation reference is the open-source \texttt{mrv-lib} (\url{https://github.com/modelguard-lab/mrv-lib}); institutions are expected to calibrate the triggers to their own admissible class. Scope: four-asset case-study, daily univariate features, fixed 252-day window, discrete-state HMM/GMM and non-parametric k-means/spectral; extension to multivariate features, higher frequencies, and continuous-state model classes is left for future work.

\paragraph{Relation to recent literature.}
This result builds on recent \emph{Finance Research Letters} work that fixes feature and specification choice and evaluates the chosen regime model by forecasting or trading performance \citep{Kirby2023FRL,BlazsekKongShadoff2025,Ampountolas2025FRL,WangYuanQu2026FRL}, where \citet{Ampountolas2025FRL} shows feature choice shapes regime assignments and \citet{BlazsekKongShadoff2025} documents score-driven specification sensitivity. We extend that line by promoting cross-representation \emph{label-partition} agreement from an unmonitored sensitivity to a quantified, chance-corrected metric computed \emph{across} the admissible class, and by mapping it to an SR~11-7 escalation action, the validation layer those performance-based evaluations leave implicit.
