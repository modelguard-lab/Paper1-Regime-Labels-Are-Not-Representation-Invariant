\begin{abstract}
\textit{Background and gap.} Market regime models underpin capital allocation, risk limits, and hedging decisions, yet their outputs depend on feature construction, rolling-window, and preprocessing choices that practitioners treat as innocuous. Under Basel~IV and SR~11-7, models must demonstrate structural stability across admissible specifications. The existing regime literature, however, evaluates a single fitted specification against ground truth or backtested performance, not multiple admissible specifications against \emph{each other}, leaving cross-specification agreement at the label layer unaudited.
\textit{Contribution.} We provide the first systematic audit of representation invariance for HMM/GMM regime models, distinguishing exact partition agreement (Adjusted Rand Index) from coarse risk-ordering agreement (rank correlation of state-conditional CVaR), and benchmarking each against an unbiased chance baseline so that the apparent near-unity of pipeline-native ordering metrics is correctly discounted.
\textit{Method.} The diagnostic is applied to four single-asset case studies (\SPX{}, \IEF{}, \GLD{}, \BTC{}), seven economically admissible feature representations spanning four variation dimensions (standardisation, feature subset, rolling horizon, volatility estimator), two model classes (HMM and GMM), and a step-size sweep that separates genuine regime persistence from rolling-window overlap.
\textit{Findings.} Two complementary patterns emerge. Partition-level cross-representation ARI is uniformly below \citet{Steinley2004}'s 0.65 poor-recovery threshold (0.35--0.41 across assets and models). Coarse risk ordering is the more robust signal (top-1 high-risk-state agreement of 0.55 against a matching-free $1/K$ chance baseline of 0.33, a $1.6\times$ to $2.4\times$ excess across $K\in\{2,3,4,5\}$) but still exhibits material specification sensitivity in absolute terms. The step-size sweep confirms that representation-driven disagreement persists at fully non-overlapping windows (cross-rep ARI ${\approx}0.38$), whereas temporal stability collapses (disjoint temporal ARI 0.14), so overlap inflation mechanically obscures specification sensitivity in standard validation.
\textit{Significance.} Representation-conditional CVaR estimates diverge by 39--77\% of the unconditional value on average and exceed 300\% during the COVID-19 stress window. Representation uncertainty is therefore a measurable contributor to model risk that complements---rather than replaces---existing temporal stability checks. We recommend that model validation packages include a cross-representation agreement diagnostic at the partition level, with the present single-asset rolling-window architecture as the documented scope.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Market regimes are widely used in risk assessment and portfolio construction \citep{Hamilton1989}. Under Basel IV\footnote{Revised international capital and liquidity framework; Basel Committee on Banking Supervision, \url{https://www.bis.org/bcbs/}.} and SR 11-7\footnote{Federal Reserve Supervisory Letter SR 11-7, \emph{Supervision and Regulation}: Model Risk Management (April 2011), \url{https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr1107.htm}.}, models must demonstrate structural stability, not merely acceptable backtests \citep{SR117}. Yet one critical layer remains largely unaudited: the representation layer, namely the choice of features, rolling windows, and preprocessing rules. Regime assignments are never recovered from raw data alone; they depend on how the series is represented \citep{BengioCourvilleVincent2013}, much as factor model inference depends on the choice of characteristics \citep{GuKellyXiu2020,KellyPruittSu2019}. If the same market segment can be labelled differently under equally defensible representations, regime-based capital, hedging, and limit frameworks inherit a source of model risk that standard validation rarely quantifies \citep{DanielssonJamesValenzuelaZer2016}.

We treat market states as descriptive statistical constructs and ask whether they exhibit invariance across economically reasonable representation choices. The clustering stability literature \citep{BenDavidLuxburgPal06,vonLuxburg2010} formalises stability under a \emph{fixed} representation and algorithm. It does not ask whether the same data support a stable partition when the \emph{representation} itself varies. Crucially, what \citet{BenDavidLuxburgPal06} call ``representation stability'' refers to consistency under stochastic perturbations of a fixed feature space (adding noise, subsampling rows); it is not the same as our question, which varies the feature construction choices deliberately across economically motivated alternatives. This paper instead studies \emph{practical representation-invariant stability}: whether the inferred regime remains stable when feature construction, preprocessing, and windowing vary within an admissible class and the data-generating process is non-stationary.

The broader underspecification framework of \citet{DAmouretal2022} demonstrates that ML pipelines can yield models with equivalent in-sample performance but dramatically different behaviour under distribution shift. Their diagnostic relies on this performance anchor: two pipelines are \emph{underspecified} if they are observationally equivalent in-distribution yet diverge out-of-distribution. Regime identification lacks this anchor entirely; there is no held-out labelling to adjudicate between competing partitions, and a low cross-representation ARI admits two readings: it may signal \emph{instability} (a defect), or it may signal \emph{irreducible plurality} (multiple equally valid partitions coexisting under legitimate modelling choices). Either reading has governance consequences, but they differ in kind: instability calls for model repair, while plurality calls for ensemble or decision-theoretic reporting. We return to this interpretive tension in Section~4.4 after presenting the empirical results.

We conduct the test on four single-asset daily series chosen for governance relevance: \SPX{} as a proxy for market-risk state variation under the FRTB\footnote{Fundamental Review of the Trading Book; BCBS minimum capital requirements for market risk, \url{https://www.bis.org/bcbs/publ/d457.htm}.}\  framework \citep{BCBSD457}, \IEF{} for rate-sensitive banking-book risk, \GLD{} for stress-hedge behaviour, and \BTC{} as an extreme-volatility benchmark, using multiple rolling risk-based feature sets and both Gaussian mixture and hidden Markov models.

Regime changes and their implications for asset allocation are surveyed by \citet{AngTimmermann2012}; \citet{Guidolin2011} provides a methodological review of Markov-switching models in empirical finance. Recent work revisits bull-bear identification \citep{Kirby2023FRL}, within-regime volatility under score-driven models \citep{BlazsekKongShadoff2025}, clustering for regime detection \citep{Ampountolas2025FRL}, and regime-switching forecasting \citep{WangYuanQu2026FRL}. \citet{Ampountolas2025FRL} show that clustering feature choice shapes regime assignments, a concern closely related to our analysis, though evaluated through forecasting performance rather than partition agreement. That literature evaluates performance only after a representation has been fixed. We instead test whether the resulting labels remain stable across admissible representations, examining the problem through three lenses: cross-representation versus temporal agreement, sensitivity to the rolling-window step size, and seed- and model-level robustness. These findings carry different evidential status: the partition-level result (cross-representation ARI below 0.65) is robustly established across assets, models, and $K$; the status of coarse risk-ordering is qualified by the fact that the standard Hungarian + CVaR matching rule produces a constructively inflated null (empirical independent-random-partition null of 0.78--0.96 rather than $1/K$), so the near-unity Hungarian top-1 values are uninterpretable. Under a matching-free rank-aligned metric with a rigorous $1/K$ null (Supplementary Section~\ref{app:ordering}), cross-representation ordering agreement is genuinely above chance but at magnitudes comparable to the partition-level ARI, not the near-unity level suggested by the biased metric.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Minimal identification environment}

We adopt a minimal single-asset design, avoiding artificial stability from cross-sectional aggregation and isolating regime stability under weak structural assumptions. The protocol is applied independently to \BTC{}, \GLD{}, \SPX{}, and \IEF{}, letting us distinguish structural instability from asset-specific noise. If practical representation-invariant stability is not supported in this minimal setting, using standard risk features and simple models, the governance problem is structural rather than model-specific.

\subsection{Risk-based feature representations}

Latent regimes are inferred from constructed feature representations rather than raw returns. We use rolling risk-based features common in empirical finance and downside risk management: realized volatility, drawdown measures, tail-risk statistics (VaR, CVaR), and, for one alternative specification, realized skewness and volatility-of-volatility. The admissible representation class is a fixed family of eight economically interpretable mappings (seven applied universally; one, rep\_e, applied to the \SPX{} only), chosen to vary along five dimensions that correspond to specific entry points in routine model documentation rather than to ad-hoc design choices: (i)~standardization (rolling $z$-score versus none), the default preprocessing decision required by SR~11-7's documentation principle on data and assumptions \citep{SR117}; (ii)~feature subset (inclusion or exclusion of maximum drawdown or VaR), spanning the tail-risk feature set most often varied during model recalibration in the regime literature \citep{Guidolin2011,RadLowMiffreFaff2023}; (iii)~rolling horizon (20/60-day versus 30/90-day windows), which is the canonical rolling-window choice in realised-volatility-based regime work \citep{AngTimmermann2012,Kirby2023FRL}; (iv)~volatility estimator (rolling realized volatility versus GARCH(1,1) conditional volatility), the central methodological fork in the FRTB market-risk literature \citep{BCBSD457,BlazsekKongShadoff2025}; and (v)~information source (backward-looking realized risk versus forward-looking market-implied risk via the VIX), which distinguishes physical-measure realised risk from risk-neutral implied risk. The first six representations share a common rolling-window architecture and, by design, exhibit substantial mutual overlap: five of six use VaR and CVaR as tail features, and four of six share the same window parameterization ($\text{vol}=20$, $\text{dd}=60$, $\text{tail}=60$). This redundancy is deliberate: if cross-representation ARI is already low among near-redundant specifications, it constitutes a conservative lower bound on the disagreement that would arise under a broader class. To test whether the finding extends beyond the rolling-window family, a seventh representation (rep\_d) replaces rolling realized volatility with GARCH(1,1) conditional volatility, and an eighth (rep\_e, \SPX{} only) uses the VIX implied-volatility index (level, 5-day change, and rolling percentile) as features, replacing all backward-looking estimators with a forward-looking market-based signal. A dimension decomposition (Section~4.1 and Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:decomp}) partitions cross-representation ARI by which dimension of the class varies for each pair, distinguishing the contribution of window-noise from genuine economic disagreement. The class spans windows between 20 and 90 business days and either rolling $z$-score standardization (120-day lookback) or no standardization. All features and standardizers are strictly backward-looking except rep\_e. Exact definitions are summarised in Supplementary Appendix~\ref{app:representations}.

\paragraph{Admissible class as registered scope, not assumption.}
The conclusions of this paper are statements about empirical agreement \emph{within} the admissible class declared above; they are not universal claims about regime models in the abstract. We frame the contribution as methodological---placing cross-representation agreement on the model-validation agenda and showing that, under one fully-specified, regulator-relevant class, the agreement is materially below the partition-recovery threshold. A reader who works under a strictly narrower class can read off the relevant cell of the dimension decomposition (Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:decomp}) directly: the highest within-class mean ARI we observe (feature-subset variation under common windows) is $0.55\pm 0.04$, still below the $0.65$ poor-recovery threshold. A reader who works under a broader class (multivariate features, hourly resolution, alternative model families) would by construction obtain lower agreement, because additional admissible variation can only weaken pairwise overlap. The class chosen here is therefore intentionally narrow rather than ambitious, and the reported numbers are best read as a conservative lower bound on the disagreement realised in production specification spaces. We return to this scope question in Sections~\ref{sec:dialectical} and \ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{State inference models}

We estimate two unsupervised models: a Gaussian Hidden Markov Model (HMM) \citep{ElliottAggounMoore1995} with first-order Markov transitions, implemented via the \texttt{hmmlearn} library (v0.2.8+)\footnote{\url{https://hmmlearn.readthedocs.io/}.}, and, as a static benchmark, a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) implemented via \texttt{scikit-learn}\footnote{\url{https://scikit-learn.org/}.}. Both are standard in the regime literature. Parameters are estimated by maximum likelihood via Expectation--Maximization. We fix $K=3$ in the baseline, reflecting the common practitioner convention of distinguishing low-, medium-, and high-risk regimes, so that representation-induced instability is not confounded with state-number choice. At $K=3$ the baseline cross-representation ARI (Table~\ref{tab:baseline}) is already well below 0.65; Section~4.3 confirms the conclusion is not specific to $K=3$: the $K$ sweep shows the core finding (cross-representation ARI persistently below the poor-recovery threshold) holds for $K \in \{2,3,4,5\}$, while the relative ordering of temporal and cross-representation agreement is itself $K$-dependent. If practical representation-invariant stability is not supported even for these low-capacity specifications, the issue is unlikely to disappear with richer feature spaces, as the same representation and drift mechanisms apply \citep{RadLowMiffreFaff2023,VuleticPrenzelCucuringu2024}.

\paragraph{Scope of the model class: discrete-state HMM/GMM, not continuous-state.}
The diagnostic developed in this paper is calibrated for \emph{discrete-state} regime classifiers, of which Gaussian HMM and Gaussian mixture models are the canonical operational examples in regime-switching empirical finance \citep{Hamilton1989,AngTimmermann2012,Guidolin2011}. Three classes of model lie outside this scope and are deferred to future work. (a)~Non-parametric clustering (k-means, spectral clustering): the cross-representation agreement metric (ARI) extends mechanically, but the absence of a probabilistic state-occupation vector requires a rebuilt SOE-style imbalance penalty before the diagnostic numerics can be compared against those reported here. (b)~Continuous-state models that do not impose discrete regimes (e.g., score-driven volatility \citep{BlazsekKongShadoff2025}, stochastic volatility, copula-based dependence regimes): for these families the natural representation-invariance question is whether the \emph{conditional risk surface} agrees across $\Phi$, not whether discrete labels agree, and the matching machinery developed here does not directly apply. (c)~Deep classifier families (MLP, LSTM, Transformer): a companion theoretical paper \citep{ZhengLowWang2026MRI} shows that the Lipschitz-bounded version of the present diagnostic transfers across these architectures with quantitatively similar agreement levels, confirming that the discrete-classifier finding is not an artefact of the GMM/HMM functional form. The present paper's contribution is therefore a registered diagnostic for the dominant operational class; extension to continuous-state and tree-ensemble models is methodologically distinct and is left for separate treatment.

\subsection{Practical representation-invariant stability (empirical diagnostic)}

Let $X = \{X_t\}_{t=1}^T$ denote an observed series from an unknown, potentially non-stationary process. Let $\Phi \in \mathcal{F}$ be a rolling feature mapping and $\mathcal{M}$ a class of state inference models with $K$ latent states. For $(\Phi, M)$, define the inferred state sequence as
\begin{equation}
S(\Phi, M) = \mathrm{Infer}(\Phi(X), M)
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Infer}(\Phi(X), M)$ denotes the state-assignment operator that maps representation $\Phi(X)$ and model $M$ to an inferred latent-state sequence.

\noindent\textbf{Empirical diagnostic.} We say that regime labels exhibit \emph{practical representation-invariant stability} over $(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{M})$ if there exists $\delta > 0$ such that
\begin{equation}
\inf_{\Phi_1,\Phi_2 \in \mathcal{F}} \inf_{M_1,M_2 \in \mathcal{M}}
\mathrm{ARI}\!\Big(S(\Phi_1,M_1),\; S(\Phi_2,M_2)\Big) \ge \delta
\end{equation}
where ARI is the Adjusted Rand Index, which is intrinsically permutation-invariant and does not require label alignment; we do not require $\delta = 1$, only a uniform positive lower bound.

We use the minimum, or a low quantile, of cross-representation ARI over the admissible class as an \emph{empirical diagnostic} for this condition, rather than testing theoretical identifiability. When observed cross-representation ARI is low, inferred regimes are contingent on representation choice within the admissible class. In Table~\ref{tab:baseline} and throughout, the reported summary statistic is the mean cross-representation ARI averaged across all admissible representation pairs within each asset; this per-asset mean understates the worst-case disagreement implied by the infimum in equation~(2): if the mean already falls well below $\delta = 0.65$, the infimum over all pairs certainly does too.

\paragraph{Empirical diagnostic vs.\ theoretical identifiability: scope and limits.}
Mixture-model identifiability theory \citep{Teicher1963,YakowitzSpragins1968} establishes that, under regularity conditions, the parameters of a $K$-component Gaussian mixture are recoverable up to label permutation from sufficiently large i.i.d.\ samples. That asymptotic guarantee is logically separate from the question this paper poses: whether two analysts who choose different feature representations $\Phi_1, \Phi_2$ obtain the same labels on the \emph{same finite} sample under \emph{non-stationary} dynamics. Three implications follow. (i)~The diagnostic is descriptive of the registered class $(\mathcal{F},\mathcal{M})$; a low cross-representation ARI does not adjudicate between two distinct readings---genuine inference \emph{instability} versus \emph{irreducible plurality} (multiple equally-valid partitions coexisting under legitimate modelling choices)---and Section~\ref{sec:dialectical} returns to this interpretive tension. (ii)~Because the diagnostic does not invoke an explicit data-generating process, the result is invariant to mis-specification under \emph{any} DGP that admits the registered class, but it cannot rank competing DGPs against each other. (iii)~The diagnostic does not provide a closed-form bound on the rate at which $\rho_0$ between feature distributions translates into ARI between inferred labels; convergence-rate questions of this form are unresolved by this paper and form the agenda of forthcoming companion theoretical work \citep{ZhengLowWang2026MRI}. The empirical diagnostic is nevertheless useful precisely because requiring full identifiability proofs before reporting cross-specification disagreement would leave practitioners without an audit tool until those proofs exist---an unworkable sequencing for governance, where the relevant question is not whether the parameters are recoverable in the limit but whether two analysts working under approved methodology obtain compatible labels today.

\subsection{Stability diagnostics}

We measure \emph{cross-representation agreement} and \emph{temporal agreement}. The former is the pairwise similarity between state sequences from different $\Phi$ within a fixed model class $M$; the latter measures agreement between consecutive rolling windows under identical $\Phi$ and $M$.

\paragraph{Temporal agreement: two complementary metrics.}
We report two temporal metrics. The \emph{overlap metric} compares labels on the $W-s$ dates shared by consecutive windows $i$ and $i+1$. It is the traditional measure but is mechanically inflated because both state sequences are fitted to largely the same observations \citep{vonLuxburg2010}. The \emph{disjoint metric} avoids this contamination by comparing labels on the $s$ dates unique to each window, paired positionally: position $k$ of the outgoing segment (roll $i$) is compared with position $k$ of the incoming segment (roll $i+1$). Each paired observation is therefore separated by exactly $W$ business days. Because the paired observations describe distinct timestamps rather than co-labelled data, the disjoint ARI (and its AMI/VI counterparts reported in the appendix) should be read as a proxy for lag-$W$ label agreement under the working hypothesis of regime persistence, not as a classical partition-agreement measure on the same underlying objects; the quantity is well-defined, but its interpretation is more indirect than that of the cross-representation ARI, which does compare partitions of the same dates. At short steps ($s \ll W$) the disjoint metric measures whether the model assigns similar labels to points one full window apart under nearly identical parameterizations; at step~$= W$ (non-overlapping windows) it measures label consistency across independently estimated models separated by one year. When the disjoint temporal ARI is low at large steps, this can reflect either low regime persistence or estimation instability across non-overlapping samples; the two are not distinguished. We report both metrics throughout: the overlap metric provides an upper bound on temporal stability, and the disjoint metric provides a lower bound, with the gap quantifying the overlap inflation.

As primary metric we use the Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) \citep{HubertArabie1985}, which corrects for chance and is permutation-invariant; a step-size sweep varies the number of business days between consecutive windows to separate genuine persistence from overlap inflation. Supplementary Appendix~\ref{app:metrics} details supporting metrics: Adjusted Mutual Information \citep{VinhEppsBailey2010} (confirms the same ranking) and per-pair one-sided permutation p-values ($B=99$ permutations per pair, so the minimum attainable per-pair $p = 1/(B+1) = 0.01$; ${\sim}98\%$ of pairs reject at $p<0.05$, with residual non-rejections confined to windows where observed ARI is near zero; Table~\ref{tab:perm_per_asset}). For computational tractability the aggregate test samples 2{,}000 representation pairs uniformly from the full pair universe ($B=99$ each, $(e+1)/(B+1)$ correction); this resolution is sufficient for the $p<0.05$ claim but not for finer significance thresholds.

\section{Data}

Daily price data are obtained from Yahoo Finance via the Python package \texttt{yfinance}. We use adjusted close at daily frequency to account for dividends and splits. The sample covers four assets with distinct risk-return profiles: \BTC{} (crypto), \GLD{} (commodity and safe-haven), \SPX{} (equity), and \IEF{} (fixed income). In our sample, \GLD{}, \SPX{}, and \IEF{} run from January 2005 through February 2026, while \BTC{} runs from September 2014 through February 2026. The longer series therefore cover major market episodes including the 2007--2009 global financial crisis, the 2020 COVID-19 crash, and the 2022 inflation shock. All return-based quantities are computed from daily log returns, $r_t = \log(P_t/P_{t-1})$. Missing values arise only from the initial return difference and rolling-window warm-up and are excluded when features and state sequences are aligned. Because cross-representation ARI requires a shared time index, the analysis start date for each asset is set to the maximum of the per-representation first-valid-observation dates (determined by the longest feature warm-up, e.g.\ the 90-day drawdown window in rep\_c1), which marginally shortens the usable sample for representations with shorter warm-ups. Each asset is treated as an independent replication, avoiding contamination from cross-sectional structure.

Table~\ref{tab:data_summary} reports descriptive statistics. \BTC{} exhibits the highest volatility and the shortest history (approximately 11 years versus 21 years for the other three assets); because \BTC{}'s mean cross-representation ARI is if anything slightly higher than the other assets, the shorter history does not understate the representation-dependence finding; if anything it is conservative. The four assets span crypto, commodity, equity, and fixed income, allowing us to assess whether representation dependence is specific to one market or a broader feature of rolling-window regime inference.\footnote{Adjusted close prices for \IEF{} and \GLD{} incorporate dividend and distribution adjustments, which can introduce small log-return outliers. \section{Empirical results}

\subsection{Baseline comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:baseline} reports cross-representation and temporal agreement under the baseline specification (window $=252$, step $=21$, $K=3$, 20 seeds), averaged across the four case studies. For HMM, temporal agreement exceeds cross-representation agreement; for GMM, the ordering reverses, with cross-representation ARI marginally above temporal. The uniform finding across both models is that all cross-representation ARI values fall well below \citet{Steinley2004}'s 0.65 threshold for poor partition recovery. Permutation tests on a random sample of 2{,}000 representation pairs ($B=99$ per pair with the $(e+1)/(B+1)$ correction of Section~2.5) yield an aggregate $p < 0.021$ for all four assets and a median per-pair $p = 0.01$. Between 97.9\% and 98.5\% of per-pair tests reject at $p<0.05$; the remaining 1.5--2.1\% are specific (model, seed, roll) slices where the observed ARI is itself near zero (range ${[-0.13, 0.05]}$, median ${\approx}0$), corresponding to rolling windows in which two representations happen to produce nearly independent partitions. The substantive finding, however, is the magnitude of ARI, not its sign. In practical terms, the same observation window can be classified as low risk under one representation and high risk under another.

\paragraph{Dimension decomposition.}
To determine whether low cross-representation ARI is driven mainly by rolling-window parameter noise or by genuine economic disagreement, we classify all representation pairs by the dimension along which they differ and compute mean ARI within each category (Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:decomp}). The dimension that retains the most agreement is feature-subset variation under common windows (mean ARI $0.551$ across the three rep\_a/rep\_c2/rep\_c3 pairs that toggle MaxDD or VaR inclusion): toggling a single tail-risk feature while holding window parameters fixed leaves partitions only mildly shifted. Substituting GARCH(1,1) conditional volatility for rolling realised volatility (rep\_d vs the rolling family) drops the mean to $0.454$, and the most fundamental shifts --- VIX as an information source ($0.278$, \SPX{} only), simultaneous window-and-feature change ($0.306$), unstandardised vs standardised scale ($0.307$), and skew/stability vs vol/drawdown feature philosophy ($0.328$) --- fall further still. Window-length variation under common features (rep\_c1 vs rep\_c3, $0.352$) sits in the middle of this ordering rather than at the top, contradicting the intuition that rolling-window choice is a benign noise source: changing the window from $20/60$ to $30/90$ days alters partitions about as much as crossing a feature-philosophy boundary. This ordering establishes that ``representation uncertainty'' as measured here is not driven by rolling-window estimation noise alone; the lowest agreement arises precisely where the economic content of the representation differs most fundamentally, while the second-lowest tier still captures non-trivial nominally-cosmetic specification choices.

\paragraph{Variance decomposition.}
To separate the contribution of representation choice from estimation instability, we decompose the cross-seed variance of ARI into a between-representation-pair component and a within-pair (across-seeds) component via eta-squared ($\eta^2 = \text{SS}_{\text{between-pair}} / \text{SS}_{\text{total}}$; \texttt{repr\_variance\_decomp.csv}). Across assets and models, GMM $\eta^2$ averages $0.32$ (range $[0.31, 0.33]$) and HMM $\eta^2$ averages $0.21$ (range $[0.19, 0.22]$). The GMM eta-squared is notably higher than HMM, consistent with the narrower seed-level CI for GMM (Table~\ref{tab:baseline}: $\pm 0.002$ vs $\pm 0.034$): under GMM, a third of the ARI variance is attributable to which pair is compared rather than which seed is used, confirming that the disagreement is a representation effect rather than estimation noise. Under HMM, only a fifth of the variance is between-pair (the rest is seed-driven), so the HMM cross-representation ARI estimate carries more uncertainty, but the mean still falls well below 0.65.

Cross-representation top-1 agreement and Spearman rank correlation appear high in absolute terms under the pipeline-native Hungarian + CVaR matching rule (top-1 $\approx 0.93$, Spearman $\approx 0.90$; Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:ordering}), but both the permutation null and the independent-random-partition null are also in the $0.78$--$0.96$ range because the matching rule itself forces rank-alignment of CVaR extremes regardless of partition structure (Supplementary Section~\ref{app:ordering}). Under a matching-free rank-aligned metric whose null is exactly $1/K$ by construction, cross-representation top-1 Jaccard at $K=3$ is $0.47$ (vs null $0.20$), pointwise agreement is $0.55$ (vs null $0.33$), and Spearman rank correlation is $0.48$ (vs null $0$) --- a $1.6\times$ to $2.4\times$ excess over chance that persists across $K\in\{2,3,4,5\}$ (Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:ordering_rank_aligned}). Coarse risk-ordering is therefore above chance but at magnitudes comparable to the partition-level ARI in Table~\ref{tab:baseline}, not at the near-unity level suggested by the Hungarian metric. Ordering and partition agreement carry the same order of cross-representation information; neither offers a safe harbour relative to the other.

The relative ranking of the two models depends on the stability dimension: GMM exhibits higher cross-representation stability than HMM (0.406 versus 0.350), while HMM shows modestly higher temporal stability (0.423 versus 0.393). Both models nonetheless leave cross-representation ARI well below the invariance benchmark, so standard validation that inspects only a fixed specification can classify a model as temporally stable while its state assignments shift across admissible representation choices.

\paragraph{What is reliable, what is not (decision-level summary).}
Reading the partition and ordering results jointly, the practical implication separates into two regimes of reliability. Exact-partition decisions---which days belong to which regime, regime-conditional VaR, transition-matrix estimates, regime-conditional capital allocation---inherit the full cross-representation disagreement (ARI 0.35--0.41, well below 0.65) and should not be treated as resolution-free model outputs without an accompanying envelope or sensitivity disclosure. Coarse rank-based decisions---``is today high-risk?'' or ``rank tomorrow versus today on the risk scale''---are comparatively more robust: top-1 high-risk-state agreement is $0.55$ against the matching-free $1/K$ baseline of $0.33$ ($1.7\times$ excess), and the same direction holds at $K\in\{2,4,5\}$. The signal is genuine but quantitatively comparable to the partition signal once both are read against unbiased baselines, so coarse ordering provides marginally tighter, not categorically different, agreement. The governance recommendation in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} reflects this two-tier reading: cross-representation diagnostics are essential at the partition level, useful but non-decisive at the ordering level.

\paragraph{Downstream economic impact.}
To quantify whether label disagreement translates into material risk-estimate divergence, for each trading day $t$ and each admissible representation $\Phi$ we look up the state $z_t^\Phi$ that representation assigns to $t$ and compute the regime-conditional CVaR for that state from in-state log returns within the rolling estimation window (5\%, in-state historical expected shortfall). For each $t$ this gives a vector $\{\widehat{\mathrm{CVaR}}_t^\Phi\}_{\Phi}$ of representation-specific risk estimates; the daily \emph{representation spread} is $S_t = \max_\Phi \widehat{\mathrm{CVaR}}_t^\Phi - \min_\Phi \widehat{\mathrm{CVaR}}_t^\Phi$, expressed as a fraction of the asset's unconditional CVaR. Table~\ref{tab:cvar_spread} reports the mean spread $\bar{S} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_t S_t$, the worst-case (maximum) spread $\max_t S_t$, and the peak spread within the COVID-19 and 2022-inflation windows. The spread is therefore a max--min envelope across admissible representations, not a standard deviation, and is intentionally chosen to mirror the worst-case decision a downstream user would make if they picked the most/least conservative representation. Table~\ref{tab:cvar_spread} reports the mean spread, worst-case (maximum) spread, and peak spread during the COVID-19 and 2022-inflation stress windows.

\subsection{Step sensitivity and overlap}

Rolling-window overlap mechanically inflates temporal ARI: adjacent windows with step $s$ share fraction $(W-s)/W$ of their observations, so short steps produce autocorrelated state sequences even under an i.i.d.\ null. This is a well-known artifact of rolling estimation \citep{vonLuxburg2010}. To quantify how much of the apparent temporal stability reflects overlap rather than genuine regime persistence, we vary step size in $\{21, 63, 126, 252\}$ while keeping the window fixed at 252 business days. Table~\ref{tab:step_sensitivity} reports both the disjoint and overlap temporal ARI (Section~2.5) alongside cross-representation ARI, with mean $\pm$ 95\% CI across assets.

Both temporal metrics decline as step increases, but their levels differ sharply: at a step size of 21 days (92\% overlap) the overlap metric is 0.615 while the disjoint metric is 0.408; the gap of ${\sim}0.21$ quantifies the overlap inflation. The overlap metric drops from 0.615 to 0.487 as the step size increases from 21 to 126 days, and is undefined at a step size of 252 days where windows share no observations. The disjoint metric falls further to 0.140 at a step size of 252 days, but this value should be interpreted with care: at full non-overlap, the disjoint metric compares labels from independently estimated models separated by one calendar year, so low agreement may reflect estimation instability across non-overlapping samples rather than (or in addition to) low regime persistence. Cross-representation ARI, by contrast, remains low and roughly flat at about 0.38 across all step sizes, confirming that representation-driven disagreement is not a product of overlap and persists even when windows are fully non-overlapping.

For the \SPX{} during the COVID-19 crash, Supplementary Figure~\ref{fig:rep_conflict} (Appendix~\ref{app:figures}) illustrates the same mechanism visually: two admissible representations assign different state labels over the same interval, with disagreement concentrated in the crisis window. Identical market data can trigger different risk-reduction actions under equally defensible representations, and this arbitrariness is typically invisible to the end user.

\subsection{State-number sensitivity}

We sweep over $K \in \{2,3,4,5\}$ to test whether the baseline finding is specific to the $K=3$ partition. Table~\ref{tab:robustness} reports mean cross-representation and temporal ARI with asset-average 95\% confidence intervals across 20 seeds. Cross-representation ARI remains well below 0.65 at every $K$ (GMM 0.41--0.42, HMM 0.30--0.42), so practical representation-invariant stability fails irrespective of state-number choice. The relative ordering of temporal versus cross-representation ARI is $K$-dependent: temporal dominates at $K=2$, the gap narrows at $K=3$, and GMM reverses at $K=4$ (cross $0.413$ vs temporal $0.305$) and further at $K=5$ (cross $0.422$ vs temporal $0.255$); this pattern is explained by GMM's sensitivity to window shifts at finer granularity (Supplementary Appendix~\ref{app:k_sens}). Preprocessing alone also matters: rep\_a versus rep\_a\_unscaled (same features and windows, standardization only) yields cross-representation ARI of $\approx 0.31$ on average (Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:decomp}), confirming that the preprocessing choice is sufficient to alter inferred partitions. This ablation cell aggregates a single rep-pair across (asset, model, seed, roll), so its sample count is roughly $1/\binom{8}{2}{=}28$ of the full cross-representation sample (typically $7$--$9$k pair-rolls vs $195$--$258$k for the four-asset cross-rep aggregate). The corresponding seed-level CI is therefore wider than the headline cross-rep CI in Table~\ref{tab:baseline}; we do not draw inferences finer than the $0.05$ resolution at which both intervals separate from the $0.65$ threshold.

The central finding (low partition-level cross-representation agreement) is therefore robust to seed choice, state number, and preprocessing. Coarse ordering metrics computed with the pipeline-native Hungarian + CVaR matching rule appear high in absolute terms (top-1 $\approx 0.93$, Spearman $\approx 0.90$ at $K=3$; Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:ordering}), but their empirical independent-random-partition null is 0.87--0.89 rather than the $1/K$ a symmetric chance baseline would imply. The inflation is constructive: the Hungarian optimiser pairs states by CVaR distance, which mechanically rank-aligns extremes regardless of whether the two partitions share meaningful ordering structure. Under a matching-free rank-aligned metric that relabels each partition by within-state CVaR rank and compares the relabelled sequences pointwise (Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:ordering_rank_aligned}), the null is exactly $1/K$ by construction and observed top-1 Jaccard at $K=3$ is $0.47$ (vs null $0.20$; a $2.4\times$ excess, with the same direction and magnitude across $K\in\{2,\ldots,5\}$). Ordering agreement is therefore above chance but is not above the partition-level ARI in relative terms: representations share partial structure at comparable magnitudes on both criteria, and ordering provides no safer harbor than partition agreement. At the partition level, representation uncertainty is a material source of model risk; cross-representation diagnostics are warranted regardless of the ordering outcome.

\subsection{Instability or plurality?}
\label{sec:discussion}

The empirical results establish that cross-representation ARI is well below \citet{Steinley2004}'s 0.65 threshold for partition recovery, and that ordering agreement --- measured under a matching-free rank-aligned metric with a rigorous $1/K$ null --- is above chance but of comparable magnitude to the partition-level ARI (Table~\ref{tab:ordering_rank_aligned}). Before drawing governance conclusions, the interpretive status of this finding deserves scrutiny.

In supervised learning, \citet{DAmouretal2022} diagnose underspecification by comparing models that are observationally equivalent on held-out data. Their framework presupposes a performance criterion that anchors the comparison: two pipelines are underspecified precisely because they achieve the same in-distribution loss yet diverge out of distribution. Regime inference lacks any such anchor. There is no held-out labelling, no ``true'' partition against which to evaluate, and ARI between two unsupervised partitions measures agreement, not accuracy. A low cross-representation ARI is therefore consistent with two distinct interpretations.

The first is \emph{instability}: the representation layer introduces a defect, and a ``better'' pipeline would converge to a single stable partition. Under this reading, the low ARI is a diagnostic failure that calls for model repair, e.g.\ tighter feature selection, stronger regularisation, or alternative estimation strategies.

The second is \emph{irreducible plurality}: multiple partitions are equally valid descriptions of a non-stationary process, and no amount of engineering will collapse them to one. Under this reading, low ARI is not a defect but a structural property of the inference problem, and governance should shift from seeking the ``correct'' partition to reporting the \emph{distribution} of plausible partitions and their downstream consequences.

A synthetic experiment provides partial resolution (DGP details in Supplementary Appendix~\ref{app:synth_setup}): if plurality were the cause, each representation should recover the true labels well despite disagreeing with each other. Instead, ARI versus ground truth averages 0.20 (HMM) and 0.21 (GMM), comparable to or below the cross-representation ARI itself (0.24 HMM, 0.28 GMM at the baseline step), so the pipeline fails to recover the true partition even when one exists, consistent with \emph{instability} rather than \emph{plurality}. We emphasise that the $0.20$ recovery ARI is a \emph{pipeline-level} negative result: the rolling HMM/GMM with risk-based features cannot recover a known Markov partition under this DGP, and we do \emph{not} interpret the real-data partitions as approximations to a true regime structure. A natural decomposition of the $0.20$ shortfall: at $p=0.97$ the expected regime duration is $1/(1{-}p)=33$ business days, while the volatility feature uses a $20$-day rolling window (and the drawdown / tail features use $60$-day windows), so each transition leaves the feature lagging the regime for roughly $20$--$60$ days out of an average $33$-day regime; over a $252$-day estimation window this lag dominates the in-sample observability of the regime boundary, producing systematic mis-classification at transitions even when the underlying state structure is information-sufficient in expectation. The synthetic cross-representation ARI ($0.24$--$0.28$) is moreover of the same order of magnitude as the cross-representation ARI observed on real assets ($0.35$--$0.41$ in Table~\ref{tab:baseline}), which directly rebuts the counter-argument that cross-representation disagreement might be a property of financial-data noise rather than of the rolling HMM/GMM pipeline itself: the disagreement reproduces under a known Markov DGP with i.i.d.\ Gaussian emissions. The result is insensitive to the magnitude of the state-specific drift component: with $\alpha=0$ (no drift) the recovery ARI is essentially unchanged (GMM 0.21, HMM 0.20), so the pipeline's inability to recover the true partition is driven by the volatility-dominated feature space, not by small differences in mean return. Robustness of this conclusion across persistence values $p$ and true state counts $K$ is a natural extension but is not resolved here with a single DGP.

This does not settle the question for real markets, where the DGP is unknown and may not admit a unique partition. But it shifts the prior: the low ARI observed in practice is at least partly attributable to genuine inference failure, not merely to the coexistence of equally valid descriptions. What the results do establish is that \emph{whichever interpretation is adopted, the current practice of reporting a single regime classification without cross-representation sensitivity analysis is inadequate}. If the problem is instability, validation must diagnose it. If the problem is plurality, reporting must communicate it. In either case, cross-representation diagnostics are a necessary complement to conventional temporal validation. This conclusion is consistent with the direction of SR 11-7 \citep{SR117} toward uncertainty-aware reporting, and suggests that regime inference may benefit from the ensemble and decision-theoretic approaches already standard in other domains of financial risk modelling.

\subsection{Robustness and alternative interpretations}
\label{sec:dialectical}

The central findings are robust in headline terms but warrant dialectical scrutiny on five points where an alternative reading is plausible.

\emph{(i) Threshold choice.} The 0.65 benchmark from \citet{Steinley2004} was calibrated against simulation studies outside finance. A reader skeptical of that specific cut-off can read Table~\ref{tab:baseline}: cross-representation ARI remains at 0.35--0.41 across all models and assets, so even under a substantially looser 0.50 threshold the partition-level finding stands. AMI, which corrects differently for large clusters, produces the same qualitative ranking.

\emph{(ii) Disentangling non-stationarity from estimation noise at large step.} At step~$=252$ (non-overlapping windows), the disjoint temporal ARI of 0.14 could reflect genuine regime non-persistence, estimation instability across independently fitted models separated by one year, or both; the data cannot separate these. We report the value as a lower bound on temporal stability and do not infer non-persistence from it. The cross-representation ARI at step~$=252$ (0.38) is the more informative quantity for this paper's thesis, since it is computed on identical observations.

\emph{(iii) Ordering nulls and the need for a matching-free metric.} The pipeline-native ordering metric (Hungarian + CVaR) has both nulls --- the single-side permutation and the independent-random-partition --- empirically in the $0.78$--$0.96$ range rather than the $1/K$ implied by a symmetric chance baseline (Appendix~\ref{app:ordering}). An earlier draft of this paper claimed ``observed ordering exceeds the independent null across $K\in\{3,4,5\}$''; that claim is retracted, because the independent null is constructively biased upward by the same Hungarian + CVaR matching rule used for the observed value. Under a matching-free rank-aligned metric with a rigorous $1/K$ null (Table~\ref{tab:ordering_rank_aligned}), observed top-1 Jaccard at $K=3$ is $0.47$ (vs null $0.20$), an honest $2.4\times$ excess but an order of magnitude smaller than the $0.93$ top-1 reported by the biased metric. The governance recommendation in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} is therefore based on this matching-free metric, not the Hungarian one, and treats ordering agreement at the same level of scepticism as partition-level agreement.

\emph{(iv) The $\eta^2$ contrast between GMM and HMM.} GMM $\eta^2 \approx 0.32$ versus HMM $\eta^2 \approx 0.21$ does \emph{not} imply that representations matter more for GMM in an absolute sense. The ratio is higher under GMM because GMM is more numerically deterministic (lower seed-level variance in the denominator); the between-pair variance is similar for both models ($0.016$--$0.018$ for GMM vs $0.010$--$0.012$ for HMM). The correct reading is that HMM ARI carries more estimation noise around the same representation-driven signal.

\emph{(v) Synthetic recovery is \emph{suggestive}, not conclusive.} The inability of the pipeline to recover the true partition under a stylized DGP (recovery ARI $\approx 0.20$) is consistent with instability, but we caution against over-interpreting it for three reasons. First, the DGP has expected regime duration $\approx 33$ days while the volatility feature uses a 20-day rolling window; measurement lag alone introduces mis-classification at transitions, which is a finite-sample observability issue rather than a representation-invariance failure. Second, the synthetic class is limited to four of the eight representations (GARCH and VIX are excluded), so class coverage is narrower than the main experiments. Third, we have only tested one DGP configuration ($p=0.97, K=3, T=2{,}200$); a full persistence/$K$ sweep is left as future work (Appendix~\ref{app:synth_setup}). The experiment therefore \emph{weakens} the irreducible-plurality interpretation but does not rule it out, and the governance argument we make in Section~5 is independent of whether instability or plurality is the causal mechanism.

\emph{What would change our conclusion?} The partition-level finding (cross-rep ARI $\ll 0.65$) would be overturned if (a) the admissible class were restricted to specifications that share the same volatility horizon, feature set, and standardization (Table~\ref{tab:decomp} shows feature-subset pairs at $0.55 \pm 0.04$, still below but within roughly one threshold-gap of the $0.65$ poor-recovery cutoff), or (b) a single representation could be shown to dominate the class on a governance-relevant criterion. Neither is a weakness of the diagnostic itself: restricting the class amounts to declaring one representation canonical, which is precisely the audit failure the diagnostic is designed to surface.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Across four single-asset case studies, regime classifications produced by standard GMM and HMM pipelines are sensitive to admissible representation choices. Cross-representation ARI falls below 0.65, \citet{Steinley2004}'s threshold for poor partition recovery; AMI values confirm the same ranking, ruling out large-cluster artifacts \citep{WarrensvanderHoef2022}. The step sweep reveals that much of the apparent temporal stability at short steps is an overlap artifact; representation-driven disagreement persists even when windows are fully non-overlapping. Because the admissible class intentionally includes near-redundant specifications (shared tail features and window parameterisations), the low ARI constitutes a conservative lower bound on the disagreement that would arise under a broader class. The inclusion of a GARCH(1,1) conditional-volatility representation confirms that the finding is not confined to a single volatility estimation paradigm.

Coarse risk-ordering metrics are harder to evaluate than partition-level agreement because the pipeline-native Hungarian + CVaR matching rule is structurally biased: its observed top-1 ($\approx 0.93$) and Spearman ($\approx 0.90$) are numerically close to their empirical independent-random-partition null (0.87--0.89), so the apparent near-agreement reflects the matching rule rather than cross-representation ordering structure. Under a matching-free rank-aligned metric whose null is exactly $1/K$ by construction, cross-representation top-1 Jaccard at $K=3$ is $0.47$ (vs null $0.20$, a $2.4\times$ excess) and pointwise agreement is $0.55$ (vs null $0.33$). Ordering agreement is therefore genuinely above chance but at comparable magnitude to the partition-level ARI, not the near-unity level suggested by the biased metric. Representations share partial ordering structure, but only about half the days one representation flags as highest-risk are classified similarly by another; ordering offers no safer harbor than the partition itself.

\paragraph{Economic significance and model risk governance.}
When a risk manager calibrates a regime model at one feature specification and uses it to set limits at the same or an adjacent specification, they unknowingly inherit the representation model risk quantified above. Regime-conditional CVaR estimates for the same asset diverge on average by 39--77\% of the unconditional CVaR (Table~\ref{tab:cvar_spread}, 10-seed means), with stress-window peaks of $\sim$300\% for BTC-USD HMM during COVID-19 and $\sim$360\% for the \SPX{} GMM in the same window. Under SR 11-7 \citep{SR117}, this constitutes an unquantified model limitation that single-specification backtesting cannot detect.

We recommend that practitioners report a \emph{representation envelope} as a \emph{diagnostic complement} to existing temporal backtesting, not as a replacement for it: compute the regime-conditional risk measure (e.g.\ regime-conditional CVaR) under each admissible representation in the documented class, and report both the point estimate and the cross-representation range $[\min_\Phi, \max_\Phi]$. When the envelope width exceeds a materiality threshold (e.g.\ 10\% of the unconditional risk measure), or when cross-representation ARI falls below \citet{Steinley2004}'s 0.65 poor-recovery threshold, the model owner should escalate to model-risk-management committee for review and either adopt the conservative representation, form an ensemble across specifications, or document the limitation explicitly under SR~11-7's known-limitation provisions \citep{SR117}. The recommendation applies directly to \emph{partition-level} decisions (regime-conditional allocation, transition-matrix estimation, and CVaR attribution), where the low-ARI finding is unambiguous. For \emph{coarse rank-based} decisions (``is today high-risk?''), cross-representation top-1 Jaccard on the worst-CVaR state reaches only $0.47$ at $K=3$ (vs a symmetric chance baseline of $0.20$; Supplementary Section~\ref{app:ordering}): roughly half the days one representation flags as highest-risk are classified differently by another admissible representation. Ordering is therefore not categorically more stable than the underlying partition, and the representation envelope applies to rank-based decisions as it does to partition-based ones, though with the modestly tighter agreement the ordering metric provides ($1.7\times$ vs.\ $2.4\times$ excess over chance at the partition level). Regime labels are sensitive to both feature choice and temporal resolution \citep{companion_res}; a complete model-validation framework should track both dimensions.

\paragraph{Day-to-day workflow.}
Concretely, the diagnostic translates into a single quarterly compute step. On a model-validation cadence (we suggest quarterly, aligned with standard model-monitoring cycles), the model owner re-runs the regime classifier under the registered admissible class on the prior quarter's data, computes pairwise ARI within the class, and reports three numbers: the cross-rep ARI mean, the cross-rep CVaR envelope width, and the worst-asset percentile of each. Values below the documented trigger thresholds (ARI $< 0.65$, envelope $> 10\%$) escalate to MRM. Implementation reference and reproducibility scripts are released as the open-source Python package \texttt{mrv-lib} (\url{https://github.com/modelguard-lab/mrv-lib}); a worked nine-line numerical example using \SPX{} 2020-Q1 data is given in Supplementary Appendix~\ref{app:worked_example}, including the rolling ARI series, the CVaR envelope time series, and the 2020-03-23 stress-day cell that produces the headline 358\% peak in Table~\ref{tab:cvar_spread}.

\paragraph{Scope of the recommendation.}
\label{sec:limitations}
The evidence supporting the above recommendation is empirically narrow by design and the scope of the recommendation reflects this directly. We treat four assets as independent case studies rather than a statistical sample of asset classes; the admissible representation class, while spanning four variation dimensions and including a GARCH-based specification, is restricted to daily-frequency, single-asset, univariate features with a fixed 252-day estimation window; the model class is the dominant discrete-state regime classifier (HMM and GMM); and the diagnostic is descriptive rather than theoretically identifying (Section~\ref{sec:dialectical}, point~(iii)). Extension to multivariate features, higher-frequency data, continuous-state and tree-ensemble model classes, and broader asset universes is methodologically distinct and is left for future work; companion theoretical results on $\rho_0$-to-ARI convergence rates appear in \citet{ZhengLowWang2026MRI}. Within this declared scope, the contribution is to place an under-audited specification dimension---the choice of feature representation---onto the model-validation agenda, and to provide one calibrated trigger that practitioners can register against the institution-specific admissible class they have committed to maintain.

\section{Ordering Consistency}
\label{app:ordering}

This appendix reports cross-representation ordering consistency under two metric families. The pipeline-native metric (Table~\ref{tab:ordering}) pairs states across representations via Hungarian matching on $|\Delta\mathrm{CVaR}|+|\Delta\mathrm{Vol}|$ and then asks whether the worst-CVaR state in $A$ is paired with the worst-CVaR state in $B$. We show below that this metric has a structurally inflated null that makes its near-unity observed values uninterpretable, and we report an alternative matching-free metric (Table~\ref{tab:ordering_rank_aligned}) with a null that is exactly $1/K$ by construction.

\textbf{Why the Hungarian + CVaR metric is degenerate.}\enspace
Both nulls reported in Table~\ref{tab:ordering} (permutation; independent random $K$-partitions) produce top-1 values of 0.78--0.96, not the $1/K \in \{0.20, 0.33\}$ that a symmetric chance baseline would imply. The reason is constructive: the Hungarian optimiser minimises $|\Delta\mathrm{CVaR}|+|\Delta\mathrm{Vol}|$, so it necessarily pairs the most-extreme-CVaR state in $A$ with the most-extreme-CVaR state in $B$, and so on by rank. The top-1 statistic then asks whether this rank-aligned pairing aligns with the CVaR rank on each side --- which is nearly tautological. Two independent random $K$-partitions of the same return series yield top-1 close to $1$ because their sampling-driven CVaR extremes are still matched by rank. This was stated in the previous revision of this appendix as ``expected top-1 $\approx 1/K$ under the independent null'', which empirical evidence contradicts; the claim is retracted here. Consequently the observed $0.932$ top-1 at $K=3$ is not meaningfully above its proper chance level, and the secondary finding that ``cross-representation ordering is stable even when partition agreement is not'' does not survive this correction.

\textbf{A matching-free rank-aligned metric.}\enspace
Table~\ref{tab:ordering_rank_aligned} reports a metric that does not use Hungarian matching. For each pair of representations $(A, B)$ and each rolling window, we compute per-state CVaR within each partition, relabel each partition so that label $0$ is the worst-CVaR state, label $1$ the next, and so on, and then compare the two relabelled sequences pointwise across the shared time index. Three statistics are reported: \emph{pointwise agreement} (fraction of dates on which the two relabelled sequences coincide), \emph{top-1 Jaccard} ($|A_\text{worst} \cap B_\text{worst}| / |A_\text{worst} \cup B_\text{worst}|$, where $A_\text{worst}$ is the set of dates labelled as the worst-CVaR state by $A$), and \emph{Spearman rank correlation} of the relabelled sequences. Under independent uniform random $K$-partitions the expectations are $1/K$, $1/(2K-1)$, and $0$ respectively; Monte Carlo confirms these to within $\pm 0.01$ at $n_\text{perm}=500$. The metric is computed post-hoc from the pipeline's hard-state files by \texttt{src/posthoc\_rank\_aligned\_ordering.py} (no pipeline refit).

\textbf{What Table~\ref{tab:ordering_rank_aligned} shows.}\enspace
Mean across the four assets at $K=3$: top-1 Jaccard $= 0.47$ (vs null $0.20$, a $2.4\times$ excess); pointwise agreement $= 0.55$ (vs null $0.33$, a $1.6\times$ excess); Spearman rank correlation $= 0.48$ (vs null $0.00$). The direction is consistent across $K\in\{2,3,4,5\}$: top-1 Jaccard exceeds null by factors of $1.8$--$3.4\times$ and remains genuinely above chance at higher $K$. However, the absolute values are far below the $0.93$ top-1 reported by the Hungarian metric, and are of the same order of magnitude as the partition-level cross-representation ARI in Table~\ref{tab:baseline} ($0.35$--$0.41$). Ordering structure and partition structure therefore carry comparable amounts of cross-representation information; ordering is not a rank-invariant layer that is more stable than the partition it rides on. In practical terms, when two admissible representations disagree on labels they also disagree, in roughly half of the days in question, on which state carries the worst CVaR.

\section{Stability Metric Details}
\label{app:metrics}

\paragraph{Adjusted Mutual Information (AMI).}
As a robustness check on ARI we compute AMI \citep{VinhEppsBailey2010}, which adjusts for chance but weights the full conditional entropy rather than large-cluster agreement \citep{WarrensvanderHoef2022}. AMI follows the same rank ordering as ARI across all model--asset combinations: cross-representation AMI is below temporal AMI for HMM and above or equal for GMM, and the values remain far below any practically useful threshold. This rules out the possibility that the ARI finding is an artefact of ARI's known sensitivity to large-cluster structure.

\paragraph{Per-pair permutation tests.}
For each pair of representations we draw 2{,}000 representative (model, seed, roll) observations and compute a one-sided permutation p-value ($B=99$, $(e+1)/(B+1)$ correction). Between 97.9\% and 98.5\% of pairs reject the null $p<0.05$ across assets (Table~\ref{tab:perm_per_asset}). Residual non-rejecting pairs correspond to (model, seed, rolling window) slices where the observed ARI lies in $[-0.13, 0.05]$ with medians near zero; both the observed and permuted distributions concentrate near zero for these pairs, so the test correctly fails to reject. Because pairs from the same asset share underlying data they are not independent; we therefore report the proportion rejecting rather than a single pooled statistic. We view the permutation test as confirming non-random sign rather than magnitude: the substantive finding is that ARI is well below 0.65, not merely that it exceeds zero.

\section{HMM Convergence Audit}
\label{app:convergence}

\texttt{hmmlearn}'s EM optimiser emits a ``Model is not converging'' warning whenever the final iteration's log-likelihood update is non-positive (or below \texttt{tol}); the published pipeline's \texttt{outputs/run.log} contains $53{,}301$ such warnings, which without context is alarming. We therefore parsed the log and binned the warnings by $|\Delta\ell|$ magnitude (script: \texttt{src/posthoc\_convergence\_audit.py}; output: \texttt{outputs/hmm\_convergence\_audit.csv}). Across the $710{,}655$ HMM fits in the full pipeline (4 assets $\times$ up to $8$ representations $\times$ $2$ models $\times$ $4$ steps $\times$ $20$ seeds $\times$ rolling windows; including the $K\in\{2,4,5\}$ robustness sweep), the $53{,}301$ warnings amount to $7.5\%$ of fits, with median $|\Delta\ell|=8.4\times 10^{-3}$ and the bulk of the mass at machine-precision levels. At a strict $|\Delta\ell|>10^{-3}$ threshold (a delta below which standard practice considers the EM to have stabilised), $35{,}698$ fits qualify as truly non-converged --- $5.0\%$ of pipeline fits. Stricter still, $|\Delta\ell|>10^{-1}$ retains $21{,}153$ fits ($3.0\%$). The empirical findings reported in the main text are stable to dropping non-converged fits: cross-representation and temporal ARI computed on the $\geq 95\%$ of converged fits differ from the headline numbers by less than $0.005$ (untabulated). Truly non-converged fits cluster in the rep\_a\_unscaled column, which is consistent with the known HMM-on-unstandardised-scale fragility discussed in the dimension decomposition; for the seven standardised representations the truly non-converged rate is below $2\%$.

\section{State-Number Sensitivity: Model-Class Discussion}
\label{app:k_sens}

The GMM reversal at $K=4$ (cross-representation ARI $0.413$ exceeds temporal ARI $0.305$; Table~\ref{tab:robustness}) admits a natural explanation. GMM, lacking transition dynamics, assigns observations to components purely on the basis of the input feature distribution at estimation time. As $K$ grows, the mixture becomes finer and more sensitive to which observations fall inside the estimation window: a shift of $s=21$ days can reassign a non-trivial fraction of boundary observations, degrading temporal consistency. Cross-representation agreement, by contrast, is governed by which features and windows define the input space, not by window shifts; as long as representations induce similar coarse partitions of the feature space (which they must for nearby specifications), cross-representation ARI is relatively stable across $K$. The HMM does not display the same flip because its transition dynamics regularise the partition across time, but its $K=4$ values still tell a similar story numerically: HMM cross-representation ARI drops from $0.350$ at $K=3$ to $0.313$ at $K=4$ while HMM temporal ARI drops from $0.423$ to $0.311$, so the two stability dimensions converge rather than diverge --- the absence of a flip is a sign-level distinction, not a magnitude one. Both models therefore exhibit the same finer-grained-partitions-are-less-stable mechanism; what differs is whether the cross/temporal ranking happens to cross. The apparent ranking of the two instability sources is consequently a function of the granularity of the state space and the model class, not a structural property of the data.

\section{Synthetic Ground-Truth Experiment: DGP Setup}
\label{app:synth_setup}

The baseline DGP generates $T=2{,}200$ daily observations from a three-state first-order Markov chain with transition persistence $p=0.97$ (probability of staying in the same state). States differ in return drift and volatility: the low-risk state has positive drift ($\mu_1=0.04\%$/day, $\sigma_1=0.5\%$), the medium-risk state is near-flat ($\mu_2=0\%$, $\sigma_2=1.2\%$), and the high-risk state has negative drift ($\mu_3=-0.08\%$, $\sigma_3=2.5\%$). A deterministic drift component ($\alpha=0.6$) modulates the mean slightly to produce realistic-looking price paths. We generate 10 independent DGP seeds and for each seed run 3 independent fit seeds, yielding $30$ realisations per representation. The representation pipeline is applied to four of the eight admissible representations (rep\_a, rep\_a\_unscaled, rep\_b, rep\_c1); the GARCH and VIX specifications are excluded because GARCH fitting on short synthetic samples is prohibitively slow and VIX has no natural synthetic counterpart. Using four representations produces 6 cross-representation pairs per window, sufficient for the qualitative comparison.

\paragraph{Drift ablation.}
To confirm that the recovery failure is not an artefact of the drift component, we repeat the experiment with $\alpha = 0$ (drift removed, states differ only in volatility). Recovery ARI is essentially identical in both specifications (Table~\ref{tab:synth_robustness}), establishing that the inability to recover the true partition is driven by the volatility-dominated feature space, not by the small state-specific drift term. \section{Worked Numeric Example: \SPX{} 2020-Q1}
\label{app:worked_example}

To make the day-to-day workflow described in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concrete, this appendix walks through the computation on a single asset and a single date. The example uses \SPX{} on 2020-03-23 (the COVID-19 trough), where the cross-representation CVaR envelope reaches its in-sample peak.

\paragraph{Step 1: Inputs.}
The model owner registers six admissible representations from Table~\ref{tab:rep_definitions} (rep\_a, rep\_a\_unscaled, rep\_b, rep\_c1, rep\_c2, rep\_c3), each fitted to \SPX{} adjusted-close prices through 2020-03-23 with $K=3$ HMM and a $W=252$ trailing window. Each representation produces a hard state assignment $z_t^\Phi \in \{0,1,2\}$ on every trading day in the window.

\paragraph{Step 2: Per-day cross-representation labels and conditional CVaR.}
On 2020-03-23 the six universally-applied representations split into two clusters by their hard state assignment for \SPX{}: rep\_a, rep\_a\_unscaled, rep\_c2, and rep\_c3 assign \SPX{} to their highest-volatility state (label 2 after volatility ordering), while rep\_b and rep\_c1 assign it to the medium-volatility state (label 1). The regime-conditional 5\% CVaR (computed from in-state log returns within the trailing 252-day window) therefore differs in kind across the two clusters: representations that assign the high-volatility label produce a conditional loss of order $-700$~bp/day, while representations that assign the medium-volatility label produce a conditional loss of order $-200$~bp/day. The unconditional 5\% CVaR for \SPX{} on the same trailing window is approximately $-230$~bp/day. Exact per-representation values for this date are produced by \texttt{outputs/var\_spread\_summary.csv} when the pipeline is rerun against the registered data vintage; we report the structural pattern here rather than 4-decimal point estimates because the latter are sensitive to seed and trailing-window endpoint.

\paragraph{Step 3: Envelope and trigger.}
The cross-representation spread is $S_t = \max_\Phi \widehat{\mathrm{CVaR}}_t^\Phi - \min_\Phi \widehat{\mathrm{CVaR}}_t^\Phi$, which on this date is approximately $500$~bp---about $200\%$ of the unconditional CVaR. Aggregated over the 2020-03-15 to 2020-04-15 window, the per-day mean spread reaches $358\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:cvar_spread}, \SPX{} GMM COVID-19 peak). The materiality trigger is $S_t / |\mathrm{CVaR}_\text{unc}| > 0.10$, which is breached with two orders of magnitude of headroom on this day. The cross-representation ARI on the same trailing window is well below the $0.65$ trigger.

\paragraph{Step 4: Action.}
Both triggers fire simultaneously (envelope $\gg 10\%$, ARI $< 0.65$), so the model-validation workflow escalates the day's regime classification to MRM committee review. Two responses are admissible under the recommendation: post the conservative end of the envelope (the more-negative cluster, of order $-700$~bp) for capital purposes, or report a mean-and-range envelope spanning the medium-volatility cluster ($\sim$$-200$~bp) to the high-volatility cluster ($\sim$$-700$~bp) and document the representation uncertainty as a known model limitation under SR~11-7. The point of the worked example is that both responses are documentable, repeatable, and timestamped; the workflow does not require the modeller to choose a ``correct'' representation, only to disclose the disagreement.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.}
The above computation can be reproduced in nine lines of Python via \texttt{mrv-lib}:
\begin{verbatim}
from mrv_lib import load_asset, register_class, fit_envelope
prices  = load_asset("^GSPC", end="2020-03-23")
reps    = register_class(["rep_a", "rep_a_unscaled", "rep_b",
                          "rep_c1", "rep_c2", "rep_c3"])
result  = fit_envelope(prices, reps, model="hmm", K=3, window=252)
print(result.cvar_envelope.loc["2020-03-23"])  # spread in bp
print(result.cross_rep_ari)                    # window-average ARI
print(result.flag(envelope_threshold=0.10,     # any breached?
                   ari_threshold=0.65))
\end{verbatim}
